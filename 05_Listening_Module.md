# Listening Module
**Target: Band 7.5–8 | 4 sections · 40 questions · ~40 minutes + 10 min transfer**

---

## Overview

The Listening test plays **one recording** — you cannot pause or replay it. Questions are answered as you listen.

| Section | Context | Speakers | Difficulty |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Section 1** | Everyday social situation | 2 people (conversation) | Easiest |
| **Section 2** | Everyday monologue | 1 person | Easy-Medium |
| **Section 3** | Academic discussion | 2–4 people | Hard |
| **Section 4** | Academic lecture | 1 person (monologue) | Hardest |

**After the recording ends:** you have 10 minutes to transfer your answers to the answer sheet. Use this time to check spelling and re-read your answers.

## The Most Important Skill: Prediction

Before the recording plays, you have 30–45 seconds to read the questions. Use every second of it.

**What to predict:**
- What TYPE of information is missing? (a name? a number? a place? a reason?)
- What PART OF SPEECH fills the gap? (noun? verb? adjective?)
- What is the CONTEXT of this section?

**Example:**
> *Question: "The conference will be held at the _______ Hotel."*

Before the audio plays, you know:
- You need a PROPER NOUN (hotel name)
- It will probably be said clearly and possibly spelled out
- It comes after a location reference

This prediction means you're not processing raw audio — you're confirming what you already expect to hear.

## Section-by-Section Strategies

### Section 1 — Conversation (Everyday)
**Typical scenarios:** booking a hotel, registering for a course, calling customer service, making an appointment

**What to watch for:**
- Numbers (phone numbers, prices, dates, times, addresses)
- Spelling of names and places (often spelled out for you)
- Changes — the speaker often changes their answer: *"Actually, can I change that to Thursday?"*

**The change trap:** The first answer given is often corrected. Listen for: *actually, sorry, I mean, let me change that, on second thought*

**Example:**
> *"The arrival date is the 14th... actually, sorry, the 15th of March."*
> Answer: 15th March (not 14th)

---

### Section 2 — Monologue (Everyday)
**Typical scenarios:** a tour guide, a radio programme, a public announcement, instructions

**What to watch for:**
- List structures: *"There are three things to remember: first... second... finally..."*
- Direction language for maps: *"Turn left, go past, opposite, next to, between"*
- Signposting: *"Moving on to... / Now I'd like to talk about... / The next point is..."*

**Map/diagram questions:** Label your map before it plays. Know the options. Listen for direction words and landmarks.

---

### Section 3 — Academic Discussion
**Typical scenarios:** two students discussing an assignment, a student and tutor discussing research, a seminar discussion

**What to watch for:**
- Opinions and attitudes (who thinks what?)
- Agreement and disagreement: *"I'm not sure I agree... / That's a good point, but... / Actually, I think..."*
- Topic shifts: the discussion changes subject — follow it

**The opinion trap:** Multiple speakers means multiple viewpoints. Questions often ask what SPEAKER A thinks, not SPEAKER B. Keep track of who is speaking.

---

### Section 4 — Academic Lecture (Hardest)
**Typical scenarios:** a university lecture on science, history, society, engineering, environment

**What to watch for:**
- Academic vocabulary (this is where your AWL knowledge helps directly)
- Signposting language that shows structure:
  - *"Today I want to look at three aspects of..."*
  - *"To summarise what I've said so far..."*
  - *"Turning now to the second point..."*
  - *"This brings me to the key question of..."*
- Numbers, dates, and statistics mentioned in passing
- The conclusion often restates the main argument — a common question location

**Section 4 is a monologue with no breaks.** If you miss something, do not dwell on it — move forward and catch the next answer. A missed answer does not cascade.

## Common Traps — Master List

### Trap 1: The Correction Trap
The speaker gives information, then corrects it. Always write the FINAL answer.
> *"It starts at 9:30... well, actually, we pushed it back to 10."*
> Answer: 10 (not 9:30)

### Trap 2: The Distractor Trap
The speaker mentions the wrong answer FIRST, then gives the correct one.
> *"You could take the bus — but actually, the train is much faster."*
> If the question is "How does he recommend travelling?" → Answer: train

### Trap 3: The Spelling Trap
Answers that require spelling (names, places) must be spelled correctly. One wrong letter = wrong answer.
> IELTS will often spell names out: *"My name is Joanna — that's J-O-A-N-N-A."*
> Write it as it is spelled, not as you think it sounds.

### Trap 4: The Word Limit Trap
Questions say "NO MORE THAN TWO WORDS AND/OR A NUMBER." Count carefully.
> "the central library" = 3 words = WRONG if the limit is 2
> Write only "central library" = correct

### Trap 5: The Not-Yet Trap
The speaker approaches the topic but answers a slightly different question first. Wait for the exact answer.

### Trap 6: The Plural/Singular Trap
> *The answer is "requirement" but you wrote "requirements."*
> In note completion, singular/plural can matter. Follow the grammar of the sentence.

## Numbers, Dates, and Times — Quick Reference

These appear most in Section 1. Train yourself to write them fast without thinking.

**Prices:** £4.50 = "four pounds fifty"  
**Dates:** 14th March / March 14th — both are written the same way  
**Times:** 9:30 / half past nine / 9:30 am  
**Phone numbers:** spoken digit by digit — write immediately, don't try to remember  
**Fractions:** "a third" = 1/3 | "three quarters" = ¾  
**Large numbers:** "two and a half million" = 2,500,000

## Accents

IELTS uses a variety of English accents: British, Australian, American, Canadian, and others. Section 4 academic lectures are often in a clear, neutral accent. Sections 1–3 may use more varied accents.

**If you struggle with an accent:**
- British Council YouTube channel has many free listening samples with transcripts
- BBC World Service and BBC 6 Minute English (free podcasts) — excellent for British English
- TED Talks — American/international academic English

## Active Listening Practice (Outside of Mock Tests)

The best way to improve Listening between mock tests is **deliberate listening** — not background music.

| Activity | Benefit | Time |
|---|---|---|
| BBC 6 Minute English (podcast) | Academic vocabulary in natural speech | 6 min/day |
| TED Talks (with subtitles off first, then check) | Academic lecture format, Section 4 preparation | 15 min |
| Dictation practice (write what you hear) | Spelling + accuracy under pressure | 10 min |
| Cambridge IELTS practice tests (timed) | Real exam conditions | 50 min |

**Dictation exercise:** Play any 2-minute audio clip. Pause every 15 seconds and write exactly what was said. Check against the transcript. This is one of the highest-ROI listening exercises you can do.
