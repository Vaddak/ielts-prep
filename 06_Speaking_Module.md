# Speaking Module
**Target: Band 7.5–8 | 3 parts · 11–14 minutes · face-to-face with an examiner**

---

## Overview

Speaking is the only section done face-to-face with a human examiner. It is recorded. The examiner follows a strict script — they are friendly but neutral.

| Part | Duration | Format | What it tests |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Part 1** | 4–5 min | Examiner asks about familiar topics | Fluency, basic vocabulary, natural speech |
| **Part 2** | 3–4 min | You speak alone from a cue card | Extended speech, organisation, vocabulary range |
| **Part 3** | 4–5 min | Discussion of abstract topics related to Part 2 | Critical thinking, argument, academic vocabulary |

---

## How Speaking is Scored

| Criterion | What it means | Your lever |
|---|---|---|
| **Fluency & Coherence** | Speaks without long pauses, ideas connect naturally | Extend your answers |
| **Lexical Resource** | Uses varied, precise vocabulary — not just safe words | Replace basic words |
| **Grammatical Range** | Uses complex structures naturally | Mix sentence types |
| **Pronunciation** | Clear and intelligible — accent is irrelevant | Don't over-worry this |

**The biggest mistake B2 speakers make:** giving SHORT answers. Every short answer is a missed opportunity to demonstrate vocabulary and fluency.

## PART 1 — Familiar Topics

**Format:** The examiner asks 3 sets of questions on everyday topics (home, work/study, hobbies, transport, food, technology, etc.)

**What Band 7+ looks like:**
- Answers are 2–4 sentences
- Each answer has a reason or small detail
- Vocabulary is specific, not generic
- Sounds natural, not rehearsed

**What to avoid:**
- One-word or one-sentence answers
- Over-rehearsed, memorised speeches (examiners can tell and penalise it)
- Starting every answer with "I think that..."

---

### The IFER Formula for Part 1

**I**dea → **F**eel/describe → **E**xample or reason → **R**elate back (optional)

**Question:** *"Do you enjoy cooking?"*

**Band 5 answer:**
> *"Yes, I enjoy cooking. I cook every day."*

**Band 7+ answer (using IFER):**
> *"I really enjoy it, actually. There's something quite satisfying about preparing a meal from scratch — especially when you're cooking for other people. I tend to make simple dishes during the week, but at weekends I like experimenting with recipes I find online. It's a good way to switch off after work."*

Notice:
- 4 sentences, flows naturally
- Vocabulary: *from scratch, experimenting, switch off* — specific, not basic
- Not rehearsed — sounds like real speech

---

### Part 1 Common Topics — Preparation Bank

For each topic, prepare 3–5 sentences you could use naturally. Don't memorise — just familiarise.

**Home & Living**
- Describe where you live / what you like about it
- Urban vs rural living preferences
- How your neighbourhood has changed

**Work & Study**
- What you do / why you chose your field
- What skills your work requires
- What you find challenging or rewarding

**Technology**
- How often you use your phone
- Whether technology makes life easier or harder
- How technology has changed your field (engineering — great opportunity)

**Free Time & Hobbies**
- What you do to relax
- Sports, music, reading preferences
- How much free time you have

**Travel**
- Where you've been / want to go
- How you prefer to travel
- Differences between travelling alone and with others

**Food**
- Local vs international cuisine
- Cooking habits
- How food culture is changing

## PART 2 — The Long Turn (Cue Card)

**Format:**
- Examiner gives you a card with a topic and 3–4 bullet points
- 1 minute to prepare (you can take notes)
- Speak for 1–2 minutes (examiner stops you at 2 minutes)
- 1–2 follow-up questions after

**Example cue card:**
> *Describe a piece of technology that has been useful to you.*
> *You should say:*
> *— what it is*
> *— how you use it*
> *— why it is useful*
> *And explain how it has changed the way you work or study.*

---

### The WHEN-WHERE-WHO-WHY Structure

Use your 1 minute of preparation to write 4 short notes — one per bullet point. Don't write sentences, just keywords.

**Structure template:**
```
Opening:     What it is + general impression
Point 1:     First bullet point
Point 2:     Second bullet point  
Point 3:     Third bullet point
Closing:     Why it matters / personal reflection
```

**Band 7+ cue card answer (annotated):**

> *The piece of technology I'd like to talk about is simulation software — specifically, the kind used in electrical engineering for circuit design and testing.*

*[Opens with a clear, specific answer — not "well, I want to talk about..."]*

> *I use it regularly in my work. What's particularly useful is that it allows you to model a circuit virtually before building a physical prototype — which saves a considerable amount of time and resources. For example, you can run thousands of simulations under different conditions to identify potential failure points before any actual components are involved.*

*[Develops the main point with specific technical detail — vocabulary: model, prototype, simulate, failure points]*

> *What I find most valuable, though, is not just the time it saves, but the way it changes how you think about design. When you can iterate quickly and test hypotheses at almost no cost, you start to approach problems more experimentally — which I think leads to better engineering.*

*[Connects to personal impact — sophisticated vocabulary: iterate, hypotheses, experimentally]*

> *So overall, I'd say it's probably the single most useful tool in my professional life, and I genuinely can't imagine working without it at this point.*

*[Closes with a clear, direct statement — natural, not abrupt]*

**Total: ~2 minutes. Vocabulary range is high. Flows naturally.**

---

### Part 2 — Managing the Time

- **0:00–0:15** — Introduce the topic clearly
- **0:15–1:15** — Cover all bullet points
- **1:15–1:45** — Add personal reflection or contrast
- **1:45–2:00** — Close naturally

If you finish too early (before 1:30), add: *"Another thing worth mentioning is..."* or *"I should also say that..."*

If you're running out of things to say, add a personal reflection: *"What I find interesting about this is..."* or *"Looking back, I think..."*

## PART 3 — Abstract Discussion

**Format:** The examiner asks 4–6 abstract questions related to the Part 2 topic. These are the most challenging — they require you to discuss ideas, not just personal experience.

**Example Part 2 topic:** Technology  
**Part 3 questions might include:**
- *"Do you think technology is changing the way people communicate?"*
- *"Some people believe that society is too dependent on technology. Do you agree?"*
- *"How do you think technology will change education in the future?"*

---

### The OEL Formula for Part 3

**O**pinion → **E**xplain → **L**ink or contrast

**Question:** *"Do you think young people use technology too much?"*

**Band 5 answer:**
> *"Yes, I think so. Young people use phones all the time. It is a problem."*

**Band 7+ answer:**
> *"To some extent, yes — although I think it depends on how you define 'too much.' There's a difference between passive consumption — scrolling through social media for hours — and active use, like learning a skill or collaborating on a project. The former genuinely concerns me, particularly the effect on attention spans and social development. But I'd be cautious about making a blanket judgement, because technology also gives young people access to resources and communities that simply didn't exist before."*

**Why it's Band 7+:**
- Opens with hedging: *"To some extent"*
- Makes a distinction: *passive vs active use*
- Uses precise vocabulary: *consumption, passive, collaborating, attention spans, blanket judgement*
- Acknowledges complexity — shows critical thinking
- Does NOT just say "yes it's bad" or "no it's fine"

---

### Hedging in Part 3 — Essential Phrases

The examiner wants to hear you think, not just give a fixed opinion.

**Opening hedges:**
- *To some extent...*
- *It depends on the context, but generally...*
- *I would argue that...*
- *It could be said that...*
- *There are two sides to this — on the one hand...*

**Developing with nuance:**
- *Having said that...*
- *At the same time, it's worth noting that...*
- *The counter-argument would be...*
- *In practice, however...*

**Giving a final position:**
- *On balance, I think...*
- *Ultimately, my view is that...*
- *All things considered...*

## Vocabulary — Replacing Basic Words in Speech

This is the single most direct way to improve your Lexical Resource score.

| Replace this | With this |
|---|---|
| very good / great | beneficial, effective, valuable, impressive, substantial |
| very bad | detrimental, damaging, counterproductive, problematic |
| a lot of people | a significant proportion of the population, many individuals |
| I think | I would argue, I tend to believe, my view is that, it seems to me |
| it's important | it's crucial / essential / fundamental / vital |
| change | transform, reshape, alter, shift |
| use | utilise, employ, apply |
| help | facilitate, enable, support, assist |
| problem | challenge, concern, obstacle, drawback |
| show | demonstrate, illustrate, reflect, indicate |

**Practice:** Every time you answer a Part 1 question, notice which word you used and ask: *"Is there a more precise word for this?"*

## Pronunciation — What Actually Matters

IELTS does not test your accent. A Colombian accent, a German accent, an Indian accent — none of these are penalised.

**What IS assessed:**
- Can the examiner understand you without effort?
- Do you vary your intonation (not a monotone)?
- Do you stress the right words in sentences?

**Common pronunciation issues for Spanish speakers:**

| Issue | Example | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Final consonants dropped | *"I wor"* instead of *"I work"* | Consciously pronounce word endings |
| /i/ vs /ɪ/ | *"sheet"* vs *"shit"* (always relevant to know!) | Short /ɪ/ for words like *it, sit, bit* |
| /b/ vs /v/ | *"berry"* vs *"very"* | English /v/ is labiodental (teeth on lip) |
| Stress on wrong syllable | *"enGINEer"* ✓ vs *"ENgineer"* ✗ | Learn stress patterns for academic words |
| Schwa (/ə/) missing | *"the"* = /ðə/ not /ðe/ | Unstressed syllables use the schwa |

## Practicing with ChatGPT Voice

The ChatGPT mobile app (iOS/Android) has microphone access and can simulate an IELTS examiner in real time. This is the best tool available for Speaking practice outside of a real exam.

**Setup — paste this prompt before each session:**

> *"Act as an IELTS examiner. Ask me Speaking questions (Part 1, 2, or 3 — I'll tell you which). After each of my answers, give me feedback on these 4 criteria: Fluency & Coherence, Lexical Resource, Grammatical Range & Accuracy, and Pronunciation. Give each a band score from 1–9 and explain specifically what I need to improve."*

**How to use it per part:**
- **Part 1:** Tell it "Ask me 5 Part 1 questions on everyday topics"
- **Part 2:** Tell it "Give me a cue card" — it will generate one and time you
- **Part 3:** Tell it "Now ask me Part 3 questions related to that topic"

**After your ChatGPT session — bring it here:**
Write out (or paste) 1–2 of your answers in this chat and I'll give you deeper feedback on vocabulary and structure. ChatGPT scores fluency well; I can help you push the Lexical Resource criterion specifically toward Band 7.5.

**The combination:**
- ChatGPT Voice → real-time spoken practice + immediate feedback
- Claude Code → written analysis of your vocabulary and argument structure

## Recording Yourself — The Most Important Practice

Record every Speaking practice session. Then listen back and answer:

1. Did I pause for more than 2 seconds anywhere? (Fluency)
2. Did I use the same word more than twice? (Lexical Resource)
3. Did I use any complex grammar? (Grammatical Range)
4. Could I understand myself clearly? (Pronunciation)
5. Did my Part 2 reach 1 minute 30 seconds at minimum?

**Compare recordings over time.** Week 11 vs Week 15 — the difference will motivate you.

## Self-Assessment Checklist

### After a Part 1 practice:
- [ ] Each answer was at least 2–3 sentences
- [ ] I gave a reason or example in each answer
- [ ] I did not start every answer with "I think that..."
- [ ] I used at least 2 non-basic vocabulary words

### After a Part 2 practice:
- [ ] I spoke for at least 90 seconds
- [ ] I covered all bullet points on the card
- [ ] I did not read from my notes — I spoke naturally
- [ ] My closing sentence was clear and deliberate

### After a Part 3 practice:
- [ ] I used hedging language to open at least one answer
- [ ] I developed each point beyond one sentence
- [ ] I acknowledged a counterargument at least once
- [ ] I used topic-specific vocabulary

## Part 3 Practice Questions — Abstract Discussion

**Technology**
1. How has technology changed the way engineers work?
2. Do you think artificial intelligence will replace human decision-making in engineering?
3. Should access to technology be considered a basic human right?

**Education**
1. Is a university degree still necessary for a successful career?
2. How important is it to study subjects outside your main field?
3. What role should governments play in funding scientific research?

**Environment**
1. Who bears the most responsibility for addressing climate change — governments, companies, or individuals?
2. Do you think renewable energy can fully replace fossil fuels in the next 50 years?
3. How can engineers contribute to environmental sustainability?

**Society & Work**
1. Is it better to have a career you love or one that pays well?
2. How has the rise of remote work changed people's relationship with their cities?
3. Should societies prioritise economic growth or quality of life?
